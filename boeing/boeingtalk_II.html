A seminar on managing complex software projects:


The Zen of change, complexity and chaos


Developers burning the midnight oil trying to meet deadlines. Project managers balancing the desires of customers and the realities of schedules. QA trying to track and manage thousands of bugs in systems. These are heroic efforts of hard working, talented people that more often than not lead to spectacular failures. Yet these are the hallmarks of modern software projects.


Developing software in legacy systems is even more precarious. To these participants these projects are often like paratrooping into the middle of a raging forest fire, holding back the flames with a McCloud in one hand and creating origami swans with the other.


Spectacular failures lead to two responses: control and fatalism. Control posits that more careful planning will lead to less chaos and greater returns. Fatalism dictates that all planning is worthless so why even try. Unfortunately, experience teaches that both approaches doomed.


The problem is change. Requirement changes, technology changes, funding changes and personnel changes all conspire to inseminate chaos into the best laid plans. These changes are perpetrated by the reality that many of a projects technologies, designs, approaches and even requirements are both unknown and unknowable before the product is created.


Careful planning does not eliminate the cost of this change because change is usually unpredictable. Indeed, trying to predict change usually introduces overwhelming accidental complexity into a project. Using a chaotic development process invariably succumbs to the exponential cost of additional changes in a poorly designed software product.


In this, talk we examine Extreme Programming (XP). XP is a disciplined method of embracing change to deliver quality products on time and budget. Extreme programming uses metrics to keep the cost of change predictable and flat. It is not a revolutionary technology, but a collection of best practices. Taken together, these practices fundamentally transform how we approach the development of software.


Most important, we discuss the technologies that make this revolution possible and how it can be used by large organizations with legacy systems and processes to create successful software projects.

About the author:

Carmine Mangione has been teaching Agile Methodologies and Extreme Programming (XP) to Fortune 500 companies for the past 2 years. He has developed unique materials show teams how to move from standard methodologies and non-object oriented programming to Extreme Programming and Object Oriented Analysis and Programming (OOAP). These courses teach developers, analysts and managers how to develop high-quality, timely software without surrendering the large amount of value that exists in legacy systems. In addition, he has developed techniques to move these techniques into organizations without disrupting current processes.


Carmine has also used XP to deliver several very successful commercial systems. Carmine is currently the CTO of X-Spaces, Inc. where he has created an XP team and delivered a peer-to-peer based communications infrastructure. As principal architect of Excite Clubs, Carmine used XP techniques to create a clubs product capable of handling 100M page views per day. As Bootleg's principal architect, Carmine introduced XP and led the team that built the company's "MediaBooster" platform and delivered the product in less than six months. Carmine is also a Professor at Seattle University where he teaches graduate level courses in Relational Databases, Object Oriented Design (OOD), UI Design, Parallel and Distributed Computing, and Advanced Java Programming. Prior to this he was Chief Scientist at Redmond Communications. Carmine holds a B.S. in Aerospace Engineering from Cal Poly Institute and earned his M.S. in Computer Science from UC Irvine.
